An Overview of Models

The purpose of Plato is to provide a means of specifying implementable models of mind based on a causal approach. A goal of these kinds of model is to capture the changing network of causal interactions that occurs during the evolution of a mind when described in terms of dispositional processes. In these kinds of systems the emphasis is on modelling the structures that support the generation and evolution of phenomenal experiences. The experiences themselves are produced from a network of causal interactions set up by the interplay of a number of sub systems. This amounts to specifying the structure, interaction and evolution of causal flows.

A causal flow refers to a spatio-temporal pattern of causal interactions. These patterns are specified by combining various kinds of dissociated instances of dispositions (did's). This is just a verbose way of saying that for causal models of mind the actual physical means by which a cause brings about an effect is not important so long as the implementation of the model results in the required dynamics. Thus, a did is similar to a neuron, or a transistor, but where the focus is on how it modulates causal inputs and how it influences its' surroundings. In addition, the did also has a power of influence (or existence) that is governed by it's surroundings, and typically competes, via superposition, in a medium that is concurrently supporting other dids. Consequently, a suitably configured neural network would be one kind of support medium for implementing a causal model. In the current context, the support medium is a conventional digital computer.
